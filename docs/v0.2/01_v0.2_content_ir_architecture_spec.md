## Core Specification (Verbatim Source Body)

You're right to treat v0.2 Content IR as *the* "hard contract surface" for Forge: it's where you're committing to (a) semantic identity, (b) measurement instrumentation, and (c) evolution rules that won't silently time-travel learners or break replay.

I'm going to frame the design choices against the two future-facing themes that show up everywhere in your blueprint + build coordination doc:

* **Beliefs are forecasts over observables** (so IR must make observables + instruments crisp, stable, and auditable; and make "what changed?" machine-checkable). 
* **Integrity + governance are first-class** (exploration support, holdouts/anchors/adversarial, residual separation, safe-mode, DAG audits, taxonomy evolution, and migrations). 

Below I'll answer each question, but first: the "shape" of an IR that won't collapse later.

## The v0.2 IR should be three layers, not one blob

If you don't separate these now, you'll accidentally encode inference internals into content (and every future change becomes breaking):

1. **Semantic graph layer (what exists)**

* Commitments (claims you want to certify / track)
* Factors (latent explanatory variables you're willing to update)
* Edges (hypotheses about dependency / transfer / blocking)
* Grouping/ontology metadata (topics, domains, strata)

2. **Instrument layer (how you observe & intervene)**

* Probe families (measurement instruments, intervention modes, invariance contracts)
* Response schemas (what a learner can submit)
* Rubrics / graders (how you map response -> observation features)
* Observation schemas (the *feature vocabulary* you extract; then encoded into obs keys)
* Optional: "sensor model shape" (what factors/families connect), while keeping parameters separately versioned if you want (sensor_model_version vs content_ir_version)

3. **Delivery layer (how items are produced and managed)**

* Static items
* Forms (consumables/rotation policies; holdout sets)
* Generators (procedural adversaries; runtime instantiation contract)
* Feedback policies (attached by reference; executed by a DSL/runtime)
* Fixtures/sentinels (non-learner, invariant checks)

That layering is basically how you preserve: *inference honesty*, *measurement integrity*, and *evolution without time travel*. It's also consistent with your later v2/v3/v4 features (holdouts, anchors, adversarial generators, DAG audits, governance, compiler defenses).

Now to your nine design questions.

## 1) Commitment identity and granularity

### What a "commitment" should mean in Forge

In Forge terms, a commitment should be the **smallest unit for which you want** all of the following to be true:

* You can define a **stable claim** ("what does it mean to *have* this?").
* You can build **at least one measurement-dominant probe family** whose observation vocabulary actually forecasts.
* You can support **closed-book diagnosis semantics** (eligibility and allowed partitions) in a way that stays honest under assistance modes.
* You can attach **certification/holdout/anchor semantics** without weird leakage.

That's crucial because later stages rely on commitments being auditable, calibratable forecasting units--not just tags. 

### One commitment with rich internal factors vs multiple commitments with DAG edges

Use this lens:

**If two facets need different "instruments" whose outcomes don't cohere under one predictive model, they are not one commitment.**

Concretely:

#### One commitment (facet = factors) works best when...

* The facets share a **tight factorization** (same latent variables explain performance across facet-specific probe families).
* You want **single certification semantics** ("this claim is true if the integrated skill is present").
* DAG backchaining *between facets* isn't a primary control move (you'll remediate within the commitment).
* You can define **one commitment-level state** that remains a stable forecast target across families.

This is the "integrated competence" case.

#### Multiple commitments (facets = separate nodes) is better when...

* You want **independent credit assignment** and independent certification/holdouts.
* "Definition" and "computation" have different intervention types / formats and you anticipate **different failure modes** and **different sensor models**.
* DAG backchaining will treat one facet as a **blocking hypothesis** for another (definition -> application).
* You want your edge audits to operate at that granularity later (v3 counterfactual edge tests). 

This is the "separable sub-claims" case.

### The migration problem (splits/merges) is unavoidable--design for it explicitly

The trap is thinking "aliases" solve splits/merges. They don't.

You need **two distinct mechanisms**:

1. **Aliases (identity-preserving)**

* "This thing was renamed or re-keyed."
* One-to-one equivalence.
* Safe for replay semantics and analytics.

2. **Migrations (semantics-changing)**

* Split (one-to-many), merge (many-to-one), or scope change.
* Requires an explicit *mapping rule set* that the engine can apply during a **state_migration event** (exactly the pattern you already have in the integrity spine).

### Practical model that won't break you later

For commitments, I'd make the ID model explicitly "lineage-aware":

* `commitment_id`: **stable canonical string** (never reused; never silently repointed). It can be human-readable like `theory.regex.kleene_star`. Do *not* encode display names in it--treat it as a permanent key.
* `display_name`, `description`: mutable.
* `aliases[]`: prior IDs/keys that were *identity-equivalent*.
* `lineage_id`: a stable group key for "same conceptual region across time,' used for UI grouping + governance stats.
* `status`: active | deprecated.
* `supersedes[]` / `superseded_by[]`: for deprecation chains (one-to-one or many-to-one).
* For splits/merges: **do not overload supersedes**; use a separate `commitment_migrations` artifact (see Section 9).

### How to split without invalidating learner history

Your v0.1 spine already gives you the right pattern:

* Old runs remain bound to old `content_ir_version` -> old commitment definitions remain valid for those runs (no time travel).
* When you cut a new IR release that splits a commitment, you start a **new epoch** (new run manifest with new `content_ir_version`) and perform a **state_migration** from the latest checkpoint:

  * Carry forward everything unchanged where IDs are unchanged.
  * For the split commitment:

    * Initialize new commitments via a conservative transform (e.g., inherit uncertainty, not "confidence").
    * Optionally, map probe families: old probe families now belong to one of the new commitments; historical attempts can be *reinterpreted* only if you intentionally regrade/replay under the new IR (don't do that silently).

This keeps both replay and learner continuity honest.

## 2) Factor namespace and ownership

This one is deeply coupled to your "factor-first inference substrate" goal (leaf modes as compiled views) and your anomaly/taxonomy governance later. 

### The key decision: factors are **global identities** but **locally scoped applicability**

If you scope factors *only* within commitments, you'll eventually duplicate the same factor (and then you can't do cross-commitment propagation cleanly).

If you put all factors in a flat global soup, you'll end up with an ungoverned ontology and accidental coupling.

The design that survives is:

* **Global factor IDs** (one factor = one identity).
* **Explicit applicability** (where it is allowed/expected to matter).
* **Explicit ownership** (who "owns" definition changes and deprecations).

### Representing cross-cutting factors without chaos

I'd distinguish at least these **factor kinds** (because they behave differently):

1. **Local misconception factors**
   Owned by (or primarily attached to) a commitment.
   Example: "confuses Kleene star with plus."

2. **Relational/confusion factors**
   Explicitly refer to *two* commitments (or two factor groups).
   These are inherently cross-cutting and should be first-class.
   (You already gestured at this in the blueprint with factor IDs that reference a neighbor commitment.) 

3. **Strategy / process factors**
   Span many commitments ("scope binding error", "symbol overload", "pattern-match shortcut").
   These are dangerous because they couple widely--so you want applicability constraints.

### "Ownership" that prevents ontology bloat

Give each factor:

* `factor_id` (global stable ID)
* `factor_kind`
* `owner` (team/system: authoring, anomaly governance, compiler, etc.)
* `applies_to`:

  * list of `commitment_ids` and/or `probe_family_ids`
  * or higher-level scopes (`domain`, `stratum`)
* `evidence_channels_allowed` (critical for your closed-book diagnosis gating; factors that can only be updated from certain channels shouldn't be implicitly updated from learning items)
* `status` + deprecation + aliasing

This accomplishes two things:

* The namespace is global *but governed*.
* Cross-cutting propagation is possible *but explicit*.

### How this choice affects sensor models, updates, and backchaining

* **Sensor model structure:**
  Probe families reference `target_factors[]`. A family's sensor model is "defined over" those factors (plus any shared global nuisance factors you choose to include). This keeps the model sparse and stable.

* **Belief update propagation:**
  Global factors allow evidence from one commitment's measurement family to update a factor that influences predictions in another commitment (good for real confusions).
  The risk is "accidental coupling." You prevent that by only allowing propagation through explicitly shared factors.

* **DAG backchaining:**
  Backchaining should not treat DAG edges as truth; it treats them as priors/hypotheses and confirms with probes.
  Factors give you a *direct* explanation path: if the posterior puts mass on a relational confusion factor, your router can select discriminating probes *without* needing to reroute upstream purely based on structure.

So: **edges drive structural hypotheses; factors drive diagnostic hypotheses**. Keeping them separate makes v3 audits interpretable.

## 3) Prerequisite edge semantics

Your v3 plan explicitly treats edges as hypotheses that will be audited and updated via counterfactual tests. That means v0.2 edges must have:

* **stable identity**
* **typed semantics**
* **a place for priors**
* **room for audit evolution without breaking schema**

### Minimum edge contract that won't break at v3

At v0.2, I would *already* include these fields (even if some are "ignored" until v3):

* `edge_id` (stable, unique)
* `src_commitment_id`
* `dst_commitment_id`
* `edge_kind` (small frozen enum)
* `strength_prior` (a prior parameterization, minimal)
* `rationale` / `notes` (human, non-binding)
* `metadata` (open object for additive keys)

#### Edge kinds: keep the enum small and semantic

You don't want 20 kinds now. You want a few that map cleanly to later audits:

* `hard_prereq` (blocking hypothesis; requires confirmatory probes before reroute)
* `soft_prereq` (often helpful; remediation suggestion; not strictly blocking)
* `enrichment` (nice-to-have; not blocking)
* `alternative_path` (signals that prerequisites are not strictly necessary; used for audit design)

That's plenty for v0.2.

### Multiple edges between the same pair?

Yes, allow it **only if** edge identity is explicit (`edge_id`) and the semantics differ (or the edge applies in different contexts). The alternative is you'll need a breaking change later when you discover "the same pair has two different relationships."

But: multiple edges are a smell that you might actually want **finer commitment granularity**. Edges can't save you from wrong nodes.

### Where should audit data live?

Not in the content IR.

IR should carry **priors** and **structural intent**; runtime/state should carry:

* empirical strength updates
* audit stats
* uncertainty

That keeps IR evolution clean and makes audits replayable as part of state, not as mutated content definitions.

## 4) Item / form / generator identity model

You have three "sources of presented items":

* static items
* holdout form items (with retire/rotate/delay feedback)
* procedural generators producing runtime instances

The correct unification is: **one selection interface, three source kinds, and immutable instance binding for replay**.

### Recommendation: treat "forms" and "generators" as first-class entities

If forms aren't first-class, you'll fight your own holdout/contamination semantics forever.

A robust model:

* **Static `ItemSpec`**

  * `item_id`
  * `probe_family_id`
  * `response_schema_ref`
  * `rubric_ref`
  * channel tags (anchor/holdout/shadow/adversarial)
  * optional: signature features for invariance/non-invariance monitoring (your blueprint already anticipates this) 

* **`FormSpec`**

  * `form_id`
  * `items[]` (by item_id)
  * `consumption_policy` (retire_on_use | rotate_forms | none)
  * feedback rules (delayed feedback, etc.)
  * form-level tags (holdout vs adversarial bank)

* **`GeneratorSpec`**

  * `generator_id`
  * `probe_family_id`
  * parameter space + constraints
  * determinism contract (seeded generation)
  * invariance contract (what can vary; what must not)
  * grading contract (how to derive key / rubric inputs)

### How procedural generators fit without contaminating the common path

Treat generators as "item factories,' but **never** treat generated items as "just another item_id" unless you also log the instance payload.

You need two identities:

* `generator_id` (the spec)
* `item_instance_id` (the produced instance)

And for replay integrity:

* log `generator_seed` + `generator_version` + **rendered_payload_hash**
* ideally store the rendered item payload (or pointer) as an immutable artifact bound at precommit time

That's fully consistent with your precommit->observe integrity spine: the item instance is part of what must be precommitted (otherwise you can't prove what was presented). 

### Engine loading/selection path

Keep the common case simple:

* Router chooses an action like `MEASURE:probe_family_id`.
* The **probe family** declares its `item_source`:

  * `static_bank` (list of item_ids)
  * `form_bank` (list of form_ids with policies)
  * `generator_bank` (list of generator_ids + weights)
* The item selector handles those cases, but logs a uniform "presented item instance record."

This avoids "forms own items" vs "items contain form metadata" fights:

* canonical membership lives in forms
* items can still carry a `form_id` *as redundant denormalized convenience* if you want, but don't make it authoritative.

## 5) Response schema and rubric extensibility

You're trying to keep deterministic grading manageable *and* prepare for richer responses.

The only way to do this without future chaos is to enforce a strict boundary:

* **Response schema defines the *shape* of what can be submitted** (capture + validation + canonicalization).
* **Grading defines the mapping from canonical response -> observation features** (deterministic-first; LLM only as a firewalled parser).

### Represent response vocabulary as a registry, not an enum baked into items

You can still have a small `response_kind` enum, but the extensibility comes from:

* `ResponseSchema` objects with stable IDs (`response_schema_id`)
* each schema includes:

  * `response_kind` (mcq | slots | numeric | structured_explanation | proof_skeleton | diagram_graph | ...)
  * `payload_schema` (JSON-schema-like validation contract)
  * `canonicalization_rules` (deterministic)

Then items reference a `response_schema_ref`.

### Keep deterministic grading sane: require "intermediate representations"

For complex response kinds (proof sketches, diagrams), don't try to score raw freeform inputs.

Instead, make the grading pipeline:

**raw input -> canonical payload -> (optional) parse IR -> deterministic scoring -> observation features**

If an LLM is involved, it's only in the "parse IR" step, and:

* must output a constrained schema
* must be versioned
* must produce uncertainty signals that feed your residual separation (spec underdetermined vs sensor unreliable)

That cleanly preserves your "deterministic-first, LLM as untrusted helper" invariant and makes later rich probes possible without rearchitecting.

### The observation model is the real stability anchor

Even if response formats evolve, your *inference substrate* wants stable observation keys.

So: tie response extensibility to a stable contract:

* each probe family declares an **observation feature schema** (what features are extracted: slot_pattern, latency bin, hint level, etc.)
* the obs encoder maps those into discrete obs keys (already versioned in your spine)

This lets you add new response kinds without breaking sensor model semantics *as long as you still map into the same observation feature vocabulary* (or you explicitly version the obs encoder/hypothesis space when you change it).

## 6) Feedback policy attachment

This is a coupling risk: if content IR references inference internals ("posterior mean of factor i > 0.63"), you lock yourself into a belief representation.

### Where feedback policies should live

Make them first-class entities:

* `feedback_policy_id`
* a declarative rule set (DSL)
* references to feedback assets/templates
* optional randomization specs (for micro-randomization later)

Then attach by reference at multiple levels with precedence:

1. item override
2. probe family default
3. commitment default
4. global/channel default

This matches your staged plan: v0.5 feedback DSL execution + intervention logging; later micro-randomization and post-measurement pairs. 

### Factor-aware branching without coupling IR to inference internals

You need a **stable "feedback context contract'** that the engine provides to the policy interpreter.

Content IR should be allowed to reference only:

* factor IDs (stable)
* *coarse engine-provided signals* derived from belief state, such as:

  * `likely_factors_topk`
  * `readiness_band` (e.g., {low, medium, high})
  * `residual_flags` (spec_under, sensor_unreliable, mismatch)
  * `evidence_channel`, `assistance_mode_derived`
  * "intervention history" summaries (dose counters)

The engine computes these signals from whatever internal posterior representation it uses. That's your abstraction boundary.

This keeps feedback authoring powerful while allowing you to change inference later.

### Channel-aware feedback is not optional

Holdouts/adversarial/shadow/anchor channels have strict feedback semantics (delayed/minimal/no feedback). So bake channel-aware defaults into IR:

* either via channel tags on items/families + global defaults, or
* explicit per-channel policy attachments

Otherwise you'll leak teaching into measurement and destroy auditability.

## 7) Versioning granularity and additive updates

You want drip-feed extension. Your integrity spine wants stable version pointers and no mixed semantics within a run.

The model that cleanly satisfies both:

### Make `content_ir_version` a release snapshot (immutable), but support deltas operationally

* Every `content_ir_version` corresponds to a content-addressed immutable bundle (hashable).
* Operationally, you can *build* it as a delta from a parent release, but the runtime contract is snapshot immutability.

Then include in the IR metadata:

* `release_id` (human semantic)
* `release_hash` (content address; this should be what manifests pin)
* `parent_release_hash` (for drip-feed lineage)
* `change_manifest` (what was added/deprecated/migrated)

This gives you:

* reproducible replay
* easy incremental loading/caching
* explicit "what changed?" for governance and safe migrations

### Per-item/per-family/per-release versioning

My recommendation:

* **Release-level versioning is authoritative** for runtime binding and replay (it's what attempts pin through the manifest).
* Inside a release, items/families can have `revision` fields for authoring/QA, but they're not a second runtime version system.

Why? Because if you add per-entity runtime versioning, you risk "mixed semantics" without noticing. Your v0.1 NOMIX gate exists for a reason.

### How drip-feed interacts with learner continuity

Use your existing epoch lineage mechanism:

* New content release -> new run epoch with that `content_ir_version`
* State migration event bridges the learner timeline

  * additive change: identity map + initialize new nodes
  * split/merge: explicit mapping rules (see Section 9)

This way, you preserve replay + don't time-travel.

### Minimal versioning contract (v0.2)

At minimum, v0.2 content IR should declare:

* `schema_version` (for the IR format itself)
* `release_hash` + optional `parent_release_hash`
* a strict rule: **IDs are stable and never silently repointed**
* a strict rule: **semantic changes require new IDs or explicit migrations**

Everything else can be additive.

## 8) Validation boundary: compile-time vs runtime ingestion

You want three categories: **reject**, **quarantine**, **monitor**.

### Compile-time (IR build/compile) must reject

Because this is the only place you can prevent bad semantics from shipping.

Hard rejects:

* schema/version unsupported
* missing required entities (commitment, family, item, response schema, rubric refs)
* duplicate IDs within a release
* referential integrity violations
* DAG cycles (unless you explicitly allow cycles with semantics, which I'd avoid)
* invalid edge kinds / factor kinds / channel tags
* probe family invariance contract invalid (bounds nonsense; forbidden axes missing)
* response schema invalid (non-canonicalizable; ambiguous)
* rubric contract invalid (e.g., cannot produce observation features; deterministic rubric missing where required)
* holdout forms with invalid policies (e.g., retire_on_use but no forms; feedback rules incompatible)
* generators without determinism contract (no seeded reproducibility)

These are "stop the world" conditions because they break epistemic integrity or replay.

### Runtime ingestion must reject (fast path)

Runtime checks should be:

* bundle hash verification
* schema version supported
* minimal structural checks (IDs unique; required registries present)
* *optional revalidation* of high-risk invariants (DAG acyclicity, referential integrity)

If runtime ingestion fails, the safe-mode behavior should be deterministic (panic/guard). Your system already treats invalid manifests as fatal; treat invalid content similarly. 

### Runtime must also *monitor* invariants that can only be validated empirically

These shouldn't be compile-time blockers, but they should trigger quarantine/governance events:

* item weirdness / family non-invariance accumulation
* discriminability collapse
* calibration drift
* spec underdetermined frequency spikes
* edge audit evidence contradicting priors

Compile-time prevents obvious garbage; runtime monitors reality.

## 9) Compatibility/migration contract

This is the "you either do it now or you bleed later" section.

### Rules for additive changes vs breaking changes

I'd adopt explicit contract tiers:

#### Additive, no migration required

* add new commitments/factors/families/items
* add new edges
* add optional metadata fields
* add new forms or generators
* add new feedback policies
* add new response schemas *without changing existing ones*

#### Deprecations (no immediate migration, but must be explicit)

* mark an entity deprecated
* remove it from future selection pools
* keep it resolvable for replay of old releases

#### Breaking semantic changes (migration required)

* split/merge commitments
* split/merge factors
* move probe families between commitments (if it changes meaning, not just organization)
* change observation schema / obs vocab in a way that changes inference semantics
* change rubric semantics for an existing item_id (should be treated as a new item revision/new ID unless you accept that it breaks comparability)

### "Old IR read by newer engines" without breaking replay

You already have a pattern: validator dispatch by schema_version and normalize/upconvert for replay compatibility.

Do the same for content IR:

* New engine can load old IR by:

  * either native support for old schema_version
  * or deterministic normalization into the internal latest representation

But: **normalization must be semantic-preserving**. If it isn't, it's not normalization; it's migration.

### Migrations must be first-class artifacts with explicit transforms

For split/merge and other semantic changes, create a `ContentMigrationSpec` bundle (versioned and hashed) that includes:

* `migration_id`
* `from_release_hash`
* `to_release_hash`
* `commitment_mapping`:

  * rename/alias mappings (1->1 identity)
  * split mappings (1->N with weights or rule expressions)
  * merge mappings (N->1)
* `factor_mapping` (same pattern)
* `probe_family_mapping` and `item_mapping` (often needed for re-association)
* **state transform policy** for belief and retention:

  * conservative defaults (uncertainty increases, not decreases)
  * explicit rule for how to map "mastery/readiness" decision stats
* `transform_hash` (so migrations are auditable and replayable)

Then, when you advance a learner timeline across releases that require migration, you emit a **state_migration event** referencing `migration_id` / `migration_rule_set_version`, exactly like your v0.1 epoch lineage spine expects.

That's the mechanism that prevents time travel and makes taxonomy evolution governable.

# Closing synthesis: what to "lock in" at v0.2

If I had to pick the *irreversible* structural commitments you should nail now (because they're hardest to change later), they are:

1. **Stable IDs + explicit lineage + explicit migrations (not aliases)**
2. **Separation of semantic graph vs instruments vs delivery**
3. **Probe family as the unit of measurement invariance + calibration strata**
4. **Global factors with explicit applicability + ownership**
5. **Edges as typed hypotheses with priors + stable identity**
6. **Forms and generators are first-class; generated instances are immutably logged**
7. **Response schema registry + deterministic intermediate representations; obs schema is the stability anchor**
8. **Release snapshots pinned by content hash; drip-feed via parent lineage + change manifests**
9. **Hard compile-time rejects vs runtime monitoring/quarantine are distinct**

If you align v0.2 IR with those, then v3 edge audits, v4 compiler defenses, v2 measurement integrity (forms/consumables/anchors/shadows), and v5 depth audits/latent contexts become additive--not schema-breaking.

You're right that these three are one system. The clean way to resolve them is to treat **(observation schema, evidence channel, calibration strata)** as a single *measurement contract* that the instrument layer declares, the delivery layer constrains, and runtime instantiates and logs--so calibration (v1.1) stays additive and the v0.1 integrity spine remains the authority.

Below is a unified resolution that's consistent with the three-layer separation and with your v0.1 `precommit -> observe -> replay` invariants.

## One object to rule them all: the Measurement Contract

For any attempt, you want a deterministic, replayable "measurement frame":

**MeasurementFrame(attempt)**

* **Instrument identity**: `(probe_family_id, item_id, ...)`
* **Observation surface**: "what outcomes/features exist, and what they mean"
* **Channel**: "what kind of evidence stream this is (and what contamination rules apply)'
* **Strata context**: "how calibration is partitioned (axes and values)'
* **Encoder / keyspace**: "what obs_key vocabulary the sensor model predicts over"
* **Eligibility rules**: "whether the attempt can update diagnosis_state and/or count for calibration baselines"

Your v0.1 spine already gives you most of the plumbing:

* attempts bind to a manifest with version pointers (no mixed semantics),
* telemetry-derived assistance is authoritative,
* precommit seals the intended semantics before observing.

The missing piece in v0.2 IR is: **make the instrument layer declare the measurement contract explicitly**, so later layers don't have to "invent" it.

---

## Where each piece lives in the 3-layer separation

### Instrument layer (v0.2 IR must declare)

This is the stable "meaning" layer.

1. **Observation schema contract**

* the *canonical* set of observation features produced by grading/extraction, and
* which subset is the **outcome surface** the sensor model/calibration treat as the "observation".

2. **Channel capability + constraints**

* which evidence channels this instrument *may* be run under, and
* what each channel implies about allowed feedback/contamination.

3. **Calibration contract**

* which attempts count as calibration evidence, and
* which **strata axes** exist and how to derive them (from logs + IR tags), even if some axes aren't "active" yet.

### Delivery layer (v0.2 IR must declare)

This is "packaging and orchestration".

* Items, forms, generators, rotation/retire policies, and feedback policy attachments.
* Anchor/holdout/shadow banks are *delivery constructs*, but their eligibility must be consistent with instrument channel constraints.

### Runtime (engine + logs)

This is "what actually happened", but it must be **derivable and auditably bound**.

* router chooses an action -> precommit seals intended channel + intended telemetry window
* telemetry + engine rules derive assistance_mode_derived, intervention trace, etc.
* observed attempt logs bind to the precommit and manifest versions

## Unified resolution to your 3 questions

### 1) Observation schema versioning: family ID vs schema version

**Recommendation: observation schemas are independently versioned, but sensor models and calibration baselines are keyed to the *effective measurement surface*, not just the probe family.**

Concretely:

#### Separate two notions that are currently conflated

**A. ObservationSchema** (structured feature schema; versioned)
Defines *features* like:

* outcome feature(s): e.g. `slot_pattern`, `score_bucket`, `error_class`
* auxiliary features: latency bins, hint level used, parse confidence, etc.

**B. ObservationKeyspace / Vocabulary** (finite obs_key support; versioned)
Defines what the sensor model predicts over (and what your v0.1 validator treats as the "known vocab" via `obs_encoder_version + hypothesis_space_hash`).

These are related, but not identical:

* you can add auxiliary features without changing obs_keyspace,
* you can change obs_keyspace without changing the instrument identity,
* and you can do a breaking semantic change that *must* be treated as a new instrument revision.

#### "Does changing observation schema force a new probe family ID?"

Not always.

Use this rule:

**Probe family ID should represent the *instrument identity* ("what this family measures and how"), not the incidental shape of the feature object.**
But **the moment the *meaning* of the outcome surface changes**, you must treat it as a **new instrument revision**.

To keep this clean without exploding IDs, treat the "revision" as:

* implicit via `content_ir_version` (since the family spec is versioned in the release snapshot), *and*
* explicit via a **compatibility boundary inside the schema**.

#### Compatibility boundary you should encode in v0.2

Inside each ObservationSchema, designate:

* **Outcome Surface (OS)**: the minimal feature set whose semantics are the basis for:

  * sensor likelihoods,
  * calibration checks,
  * and "historical comparability".

* **Aux Features (AF)**: everything else.

Then define versioning semantics:

1. **AF-only additive change (safe / minor)**
   Example: add `keystroke_count`, add richer parsing confidence, add UI dwell time.

* **Keep probe_family_id**
* **Bump ObservationSchema minor**
* **No change required** to sensor model parameters unless you choose to start using AF in inference later
* Calibration baselines remain comparable on OS; you can optionally stratify on AF later.

2. **OS extension that is *refinable* (medium / major but comparable via projection)**
   Example: move from pass/fail to {fail, partial, pass}.

* Keep probe_family_id (instrument identity unchanged)
* Bump ObservationSchema major
* This likely **changes obs_keyspace** and therefore requires:

  * new `hypothesis_space_hash` (or equivalent vocab binding),
  * and a new `sensor_model_version` that predicts the richer OS.
* **Historical comparability** is maintained by declaring a **stable projection** in the schema, e.g. `pass_fail = project(OS_rich -> {pass, fail})`, and using that projection for long-horizon calibration continuity if you want.

3. **OS semantic change (breaking; new instrument revision)**
   Example: redefine what "partial" means; change rubric semantics; change slot mapping in a way that changes meaning, not just granularity.

* Treat as **new instrument revision**.
* You can implement that as either:

  * **new probe_family_id**, or
  * same probe_family_id but a **new "measurement_surface_id/hash'** that calibration/inference key on (derived from schema major + rubric semantics version + encoder binding).
    Given you already have release-snapshot versioning and v0.1 version pointers, the second approach can work without touching the attempt schema: the revision is derivable from `(content_ir_version, probe_family_id)`.

#### What happens to existing sensor models, calibration baselines, and historical evidence?

Key principle:

> **Never pool calibration or reuse sensor parameters across different measurement surfaces unless you have an explicit, auditable mapping.**

So:

* **Existing sensor model** remains valid only for the old measurement surface (old obs_keyspace).
* **Calibration baselines** remain comparable *only* within a measurement surface, or via declared projections/mappings.
* **Historical evidence** stays usable because replay binds each attempt to its `content_ir_version`, `obs_encoder_version`, and `hypothesis_space_hash`--so you can always interpret it in its original semantics and, later, optionally regrade/remap offline.

This matches your "no time travel" discipline: new richness doesn't rewrite what old data "meant"; it creates a new surface.

### 2) Evidence channel assignment: compile-time vs runtime, without breaking precommit semantics

**Recommendation: evidence channel is a precommitted, runtime-chosen *mode* that is constrained by instrument capabilities; it is not a post-hoc label inferred from whatever feedback happened.**

That sounds like a subtle distinction, but it's what keeps your v0.1 precommit authenticity clean.

#### Why "post-hoc channel" is dangerous here

If you let "learning vs measurement" be decided after the attempt based on actual feedback delivered, you create a loophole:

* you can accidentally (or adversarially) contaminate measurement,
* and then "reinterpret" it as learning to preserve invariants,
* which destroys the meaning of calibration baselines and diagnosis gating.

Instead, enforce the causal direction:

> **Channel determines what feedback/interventions are allowed.**
> Telemetry verifies you obeyed it.

#### What lives where

**Instrument layer (compile-time)**
For each probe family (and/or item), declare:

* `allowed_channels`: subset of `{A_anchor, B_measurement, C_learning, D_shadow}`
* `channel_constraints` per channel, e.g.:

  * A_anchor: fixed format, closed-book required, no hints, cross-grader eligible
  * D_shadow: no feedback, no hints, short format, rotated
  * B_measurement: minimal feedback only (optional), limited hints (or none)
  * C_learning: feedback permitted; remediation moves permitted; may schedule post-measurement

This gives you:

* anchors identifiable at compile-time (quota planning / cross-grader setup),
* shadow eligibility declared (instrument can be used as D when chosen),
* and a hard boundary between "measurement" and "teaching dose".

**Delivery layer (compile-time)**

* anchor bank membership, holdout forms, generator availability, retire/rotate policies
* feedback policy attachment (but channel constraints bound what that policy is allowed to do)

**Runtime (precommit + telemetry)**

* Router selects an **action** that includes a **channel intent** (A/B/C/D)
* Precommit seals `evidence_channel_intended` (already in v0.1)
* Attempt record logs `evidence_channel` bound to the precommit
* Telemetry/feedback logs are used to:

  * derive assistance_mode_derived,
  * verify channel constraints weren't violated.

#### "Shadow probes dynamically assigned at runtime"

This is totally compatible with precommit authenticity if you implement it as:

* item/family declares `allowed_channels` includes `D_shadow`
* router chooses D for this attempt
* precommit seals D
* runtime enforces "no feedback/hints" rules for D

If a learner requests a hint during a shadow probe, don't "flip the channel."
Do one of:

* terminate the shadow attempt (no state update), start a **new** attempt in B or C, or
* treat it as a policy violation that triggers safe-mode/diagnostic flags and blocks updates.

Both preserve the meaning of D-shadow as a clean channel.

#### "Learning vs measurement depends on what feedback was actually delivered"

Resolve this by **not allowing ambiguity**:

* if the policy might deliver teaching/remediation, run it as **C_learning**.
* if it's intended to be diagnostic, run as **B_measurement** (or A/D), and enforce that feedback is constrained.

This aligns with the "measurement ~ intervention" framing: "intervention" is a controlled design choice, not a retrospective label.

### 3) Calibration strata definitions: include them now, but as a contract (not an implementation)

**Recommendation: yes--strata definitions belong in the probe family spec now, as a minimal, declarative contract.**

Otherwise, v1.1 calibration *cannot* be purely additive; it would have to reach back and impose a stratification ontology retroactively.

But you should keep it minimal and *derivation-based*, so you don't couple IR to inference internals.

#### What the v0.2 family needs to declare

Add a `calibration_contract` to ProbeFamilySpec that includes:

1. **Inclusion rules** (what counts as calibration evidence)

* eligible channels: usually B and D; anchors optionally used for audits
* eligible assistance: typically `assistance_mode_derived == closed_book`
* optionally: exclude attempts with high spec-under-determined residuals, etc.

2. **Strata axes** (how to partition)
   Each axis should have:

* an **axis id** (stable name),
* a **derivation source** (must be reconstructable from v0.1 logs + pinned IR),
* and allowed value vocabulary (or a "reserved/unknown" fallback).

Minimum set that's safe to lock now:

* `evidence_channel` (from attempt)
* `assistance_mode_derived` (telemetry-derived; already authoritative)
* `context_tags` (from IR tags on item/family/commitment; pinned by content_ir_version)

"Intervention level" is the tricky one. You have two options:

* **Option A (recommended): reserve it now, derive it later**

  * include axis `intervention_level` in the contract now
  * define its derivation source as "delivery trace / feedback trace"
  * until feedback DSL is implemented and logged, its value is `"unknown"` or `"none"` for all attempts
    -> v1.1 becomes additive: it starts populating and using the axis when available, without changing schema.

* **Option B: approximate it now from channel**

  * treat `evidence_channel` as the coarse intervention proxy for early calibration,
  * later refine intervention_level without changing the contract.

Either way, the key is: **the axis name and meaning exist in IR now**.

3. **Calibration target projection**
   This ties back to observation schema versioning.

You should declare which observation projection calibration is scored on, e.g.:

* `calibration_target = obs_key` (full categorical distribution), or
* `calibration_target = project_to_pass_fail(obs)` for stability across richer schemas.

This is what makes observation schema evolution non-catastrophic: calibration can continue to run on a stable projection even as richer OS variants appear.

This matches your staged plan where v1.1 scores are tracked "by strata (context, intervention, assistance mode)."

## Putting it all together: the joint invariants that prevent "inconsistency at the joints"

Here's the unified set of invariants I'd lock in now:

### Invariant 1: Measurement surfaces are explicit and non-mixable

A "measurement surface" is effectively determined by:

* probe_family_id + observation_schema major (outcome semantics)
* obs_keyspace binding (encoder/vocab)
* rubric/grade semantics binding (whatever defines the mapping from response -> outcome surface)

Within a run, v0.1 already enforces "no mixed semantics" via version pointers. The v0.2 IR should ensure that **any change that alters the surface bumps something pinned** (content_ir_version, hypothesis_space_hash, encoder version, etc.).

### Invariant 2: Channel is a precommitted mode, not a retrospective label

* channel chosen at routing time -> precommitted -> logged
* telemetry verifies channel constraints
* you don't "repair" contaminated measurement by relabeling after the fact

### Invariant 3: Calibration contract references only derivable axes

Every stratum axis must be:

* in the attempt log, or
* derivable from telemetry window, or
* derivable from pinned IR tags under `content_ir_version`.

No axis may require reading the *current belief state internals* to define strata (that would couple IR to inference).

### Invariant 4: Calibration and diagnosis eligibility use the same frame, but can have different inclusion

* Diagnosis update eligibility stays strict (closed-book + diagnostic channels, per your spine)
* Calibration inclusion can be broader, but must be declared (and you can separate "audit calibration on anchors" vs "operational calibration on measurement/shadow").

### Invariant 5: When any of the three changes, you either:

* stay within the same measurement surface (safe minor), or
* create a new surface (major), and do not pool baselines without an explicit bridge.

"Bridge" can be:

* overlap sampling (run both surfaces in parallel for a while),
* anchors as a stable reference set,
* explicit projection mapping declared in IR migration metadata.

## The decisive answers, in one line each

1. **Observation schema change does not automatically force a new probe family ID.**
   It forces a new **measurement surface** (and therefore new sensor/calibration baselines) *iff* it changes the outcome surface semantics or obs_keyspace. Otherwise it's a minor schema version bump.

2. **Evidence channel assignment is runtime (router-selected) but precommitted and constrained by instrument+delivery declarations.**
   Anchors are compile-time banked; shadows are runtime-selected; learning vs measurement is a mode choice that constrains feedback--not a post-hoc label.

3. **Yes, calibration strata definitions belong in the probe family spec now as a declarative contract** (axes + derivations + target projection), even if the calibration engine isn't implemented--so v1.1 stays additive.

## Operational Integration Notes (Added)

These notes integrate repo-specific operational constraints from current v0.1 implementation and docs.

### 1) Measurement surface identity must be explicit and auditable

The highest operational risk is hidden semantic drift if `probe_family_id` remains stable while outcome semantics or obs-keyspace semantics drift.

Recommendation:

* introduce explicit `measurement_surface_id` (or deterministic equivalent hash) keyed by:
  * outcome-surface semantics
  * obs encoder + keyspace binding
  * rubric semantics version
* make compatibility class machine-checkable in IR validation (`safe_minor`, `surface_major`, `breaking_migration`).

### 2) Generator replay binding must be a hard contract

For generator-produced items, v0.1 integrity patterns imply immutable presented-instance binding.

Minimum required binding per generated attempt:

* `generator_id`
* `generator_version`
* deterministic seed (if applicable)
* rendered payload hash (and preferably rendered payload artifact pointer)

Without this, replay and precommit integrity can pass structurally while failing semantically.

### 3) Preserve v0.1 authority boundaries during v0.2 build-out

v0.2 should define declarations and validation surfaces without weakening v0.1 integrity authority:

* no mixed semantics within run (`G-RUN-V01-NOMIX`)
* precommit intended-vs-observed binding (`G-RUN-V01-PREQ`)
* canonical event/replay integrity (`G-RUN-V01-EVORD`, `G-RUN-V01-REPLAYSTATE`, `G-MRG-V01-LEDGER`)

### 4) Compile-time vs runtime validation boundary should be enforced in code paths

The reject/quarantine/monitor model is correct. Keep strict ownership:

* compile-time rejects: structural invalidity and semantic contract invalidity
* runtime rejects: ingestion compatibility and manifest-bound contract mismatch
* runtime monitor/quarantine: empirical drift/non-invariance/edge-contradiction behavior

### 5) Keep v0.2 implementation minimal but structurally complete

For v0.2, prefer "declare now, activate later" for:

* calibration strata axes
* edge audit metadata slots
* feedback randomization hooks

This keeps v1.1+ additive and avoids over-implementing v3/v4 behavior in v0.2 ingestion work.
